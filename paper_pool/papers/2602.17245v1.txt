Title: Web Verbs: Typed Abstractions for Reliable Task Composition on the Agentic Web

Abstract: The Web is evolving from a medium that humans browse to an environment where software agents act on behalf of users. Advances in large language models (LLMs) make natural language a practical interface for goal-directed tasks, yet most current web agents operate on low-level primitives such as clicks and keystrokes. These operations are brittle, inefficient, and difficult to verify. Complementing content-oriented efforts such as NLWeb's semantic layer for retrieval, we argue that the agentic web also requires a semantic layer for web actions. We propose \textbf{Web Verbs}, a web-scale set of typed, semantically documented functions that expose site capabilities through a uniform interface, whether implemented through APIs or robust client-side workflows. These verbs serve as stable and composable units that agents can discover, select, and synthesize into concise programs. This abstraction unifies API-based and browser-based paradigms, enabling LLMs to synthesize reliable and auditable workflows with explicit control and data flow. Verbs can carry preconditions, postconditions, policy tags, and logging support, which improves \textbf{reliability} by providing stable interfaces, \textbf{efficiency} by reducing dozens of steps into a few function calls, and \textbf{verifiability} through typed contracts and checkable traces. We present our vision, a proof-of-concept implementation, and representative case studies that demonstrate concise and robust execution compared to existing agents. Finally, we outline a roadmap for standardization to make verbs deployable and trustworthy at web scale.

Introduction The Web is rapidly shifting from a place where people browse to a platform where software acts (Ning et al . , 2025 ) . With the problem-solving capabilities of modern large language models (LLMs), it is increasingly realistic for natural language (NL) to serve as a primary user interface to online services (Liu et al . , 2024 ; Plaat et al . , 2025 ) . Web agents interpret an NL task and operate a browser or APIs to fulfill goals such as booking trips, completing purchases, or submitting applications (Nakano et al . , 2021 ; Yao et al . , 2022 ) . This vision of an agentic web has catalyzed a surge of research and prototyping (Qin et al . , 2025 ; Agashe et al . , 2025a , b ) . Figure 1 . The pillar of NLWeb: a semantic layer that improves retrieval and grounding for agents. Realizing this vision requires coordinated progress on two complementary directions. First, agent capabilities continue to advance, including perception, planning, tool use, and self-monitoring at web scale, as represented by recent systems and benchmarks. Second, the Web itself must evolve to be hospitable to agents, not only to human eyes. A notable step toward the latter is NLWeb (Microsoft, 2025 ) , an open initiative that wraps the “raw” Web with a semantic layer for agent interaction (Figure 1 ). Using mechanisms such as RSS (Hammersley, 2003 ) and Schema.org (Schema.org Community Group, 2011 ) , website developers can create entities such as typed objects, lists, updates, and references from unstructured content. NLWeb tooling embeds these entities and ingests them into a vector database. However, NLWeb currently focuses only on web content. It demonstrates that a semantic layer improves accuracy, completeness, freshness, and grounding for agent queries. Yet the agentic web consists not only of content but also of actions . In this paper, we present our vision for a semantic layer for web actions and show that it is both necessary and achievable. Web verbs. Our envisioned semantic layer consists of a gigantic (i.e. web-scale) set of typed functions, which we call web verbs . Figure 2 shows an example verb that interacts with booking.com to search for hotel options given a destination and dates. It is iconized as a boxed triangle. Without loss of generality, we assume the typed language is Java. There are two possible implementations: (1) if suitable web APIs exist, the verb calls them directly; (2) if the functionality requires a sequence of browser interactions, the verb uses a browser automation library such as Playwright (Microsoft, 2020 ) to execute the sequence programmatically. Developers can ingest function signatures, types, and DocStrings from their websites into the vector database, making them part of the semantic layer. With this layer available, a user agent can take the form of a chat interface that hides a coding agent at its core. Internally, an NL task is solved as code that interacts with the Web through composed verbs. Modern LLMs already demonstrate strong coding abilities. Our experiments show that even mid-tier LLMs can reliably produce solutions for fairly complex tasks. Figure 2 . Web Verbs: a function-like action layer over today’s Web. A verb can bind to a server API or wrap a client-side interaction sequence, both presenting the same typed interface to agents. Complementary to API-agents and browser-agents. Web agents have been an active area of research. Real-world web functionality can be delivered through server-side APIs or client-side browser interactions. Researchers have therefore developed API-agents (Song et al . , 2025 ) and browser-agents, such as browser-use (Müller and Žunič, 2024 ) . API-agents can perform only a limited range of tasks, since many functions involving user interaction, session handling, cross-site data mashups, or redirection require browser operations. Browser-agents, while more general, are difficult to build and remain brittle and slow (Kara et al . , 2025 ) . They depend on computer vision, webpage analysis, scrolling, keystrokes, and other low-level steps to complete operations that could otherwise be expressed as a single web verb (Prabhu et al . , 2025 ; Lai et al . , 2024 ; Yang et al . , 2025a ) . Our proposal of web verbs is complementary to web-agent research. Rather than focusing on building smarter agents, we advocate an abstraction layer that existing agents can exploit. This abstraction is analogous to the difference between a high-level programming language and machine code. It is easier and more reliable to compose complex programs in the former. The web-verb layer provides this higher-level interface: every verb is a function with typed inputs and outputs, well-defined semantics, and a stable callable interface. The layer abstracts away the distinction between client-side and server-side functions, so browser-agents and API-agents can be unified. Conceptually, the entire “browser+Web” system is a single gigantic object-oriented program. This uniformity allows agents to compose tasks as precise code with explicit control and data flow. The abstraction offers agents three major advantages. (1) Reliability: Verbs are packaged by developers, so agents no longer need to rediscover fragile internal steps on every run. (2) Efficiency: Tasks that once required dozens of AI-driven steps reduce to a handful of normal function calls, lowering perception cycles and LLM invocations. (3) Verifiability: Verbs can carry explicit preconditions and postconditions. Typed invocations can be logged, checked, and audited. Security policies and safety invariants can be attached to verb boundaries, making rigorous verification feasible. Results. We have implemented a prototype of the verb layer and evaluated it against two baseline web agents. In two representative real-world scenarios, travel planning and furniture shopping, the prototype reliably solved complex multi-step tasks. We constructed a benchmark of 100 diverse web tasks and achieved a 100% success rate with correct and reproducible results, while the baselines failed on such complex cross-site tasks. For clearer comparison, we selected 10 single-verb tasks. However, the baselines were still unable to complete all tasks. Even for the ones completed, the execution time was 2.7 × 2.7{\times} to 8.3 × 8.3{\times} that of our approach. These results demonstrate that the verb layer provides stable, efficient, and verifiable abstractions, establishing a strong foundation for the agentic web. Our code and data are publicly available at https://github.com/nlweb-ai/MSR-Web-Verbs . Contributions. We make the following contributions: • A new vision. We advocate that a global, action-centric semantic layer for web actions is a highly valuable goal for the web community. Without it, agents remain limited to an abstraction level of pixels, clicks, and keystrokes, which is unsuitable for reliable and efficient composition. • The verb layer. We introduce and formalize web verbs within a typed program-synthesis framework that composes them into semantically grounded workflows. • Prototype evidence. We present a proof-of-concept and a realistic case study that demonstrate concise and reliable execution compared to existing agents. • Roadmap for standardization and community. We identify key areas for standardization, including verb naming and registration, developer tooling, benchmark design, and security assurance. 2. Background and Motivation 2.1. Web Agents Most web agents today operate within a general perception-action loop: at each step the agent observes the current state of the environment, reasons about the next move, issues an action, and then receives an updated state (Sutton and Barto, 2018 ) . This cycle repeats until the task is complete. Within this loop, two common paradigms of interaction exist, distinguished by how actions are represented and executed. • Browser agents act on the browser side, perceiving the DOM, screenshots, or both, and executing low-level interaction primitives such as click, type, scroll, and navigate (Deng et al . , 2023 ; Zhou et al . , 2024 ; de Chezelles et al . , 2025 ) . • API agents interact through service-defined endpoints, invoking operations over structured inputs and outputs whenever suitable APIs are available (Song et al . , 2025 ; Lai et al . , 2025 ; Yang et al . , 2025b ) . 2.2. Weaknesses of Current Agents Reliability. GUI-level interactions make reliable workflow synthesis difficult because the abstraction level is too low. An agent faces too many small operations, each carrying only minimal semantics (Wang et al . , 2025 ) . For example, a button click may correspond to “opening a drop-down date-picker for selecting the departure date on Alaska Airlines”. This level of granularity is far below the semantics of natural task composition, such as “searching for Alaska Airlines flights with these criteria”. Composing such fine-grained operations is especially problematic when workflows span multiple sites or require long chains of actions. The probability of failure increases sharply, and sequences that initially appear valid often collapse before completion (Cheng et al . , 2025 ) . This limitation is evident in IBM CUGA (Marreed et al . , 2025 ) , the system currently ranked first on the WebArena (Zhou et al . , 2024 ) leaderboard. While it achieves an average success rate of 0.617 0.617 , performance varies widely across sites, and success on multi-site tasks falls to 0.354 0.354 despite involving fewer records. Availability. Many sites expose Web APIs, but coverage is typically partial. Public, read-only endpoints are common, while many browser-available actions are absent (Khan et al . , 2021 ; Zhong and Su, 2013 ) . Essential user-facing functions such as interactive login and MFA, consent and cookie dialogs, cart and checkout flows, session-bound navigation, and cross-site redirects are implemented in rich clients and cannot be completed through APIs alone (Balash et al . , 2022 ) . In addition, many APIs are designed for server-to-server integration rather than direct user interaction. They often require keys and OAuth grants, impose quotas and rate limits, and may be locked behind subscriptions or partner agreements (South et al . , 2025 ) . As a result, an API-only agent can access only a narrow portion of real functionality. Efficiency. GUI-driven agents re-perceive the page and re-plan after nearly every click or text entry. Each cycle incurs LLM inference, DOM or screenshot processing, and tool calls. Consequently, even simple goals expand into lengthy traces: bookings and checkouts often require dozens of steps (Zhou et al . , 2024 ; Xie et al . , 2024 ) , and realistic multi-site workflows can exceed one hundred. This inflates latency and compute cost while compounding error accumulation across steps. 2.3. Necessity of a Higher Abstraction Layer To overcome these limitations, the agentic web requires a higher-level action abstraction that aligns with user goals and spans both browser interactions and server-side APIs. At this level, each operation encapsulates the many primitive steps underlying a site capability and exposes a stable typed interface with clear inputs, outputs, and effects. The layer must provide broad coverage and strong composability so that complex multi-site tasks become short, well-structured programs rather than long reactive traces. Equally important, it cleanly separates responsibilities: website developers package and maintain these operations, while agents reason, plan, and compose above the layer of the operations. In the next section, we instantiate this abstraction with web verbs. 3. Verb Abstraction for the Agentic Web 3.1. Web Verbs As introduced in § 1 , a verb is a high-level, typed, function-like abstraction of an operation that a website can perform. Unlike primitive actions such as text entry or mouse clicks, or raw HTTP/API calls, a verb directly names the intended operation and specifies its inputs, outputs, and effects. For agents, verbs act as stable units for planning and execution: they can be invoked programmatically and composed into workflows, avoiding the fragility and length of low-level action traces. The verb layer unifies browser-side interactions and server-side APIs. Implementation details are hidden behind a common typed interface.
