Title: BiManiBench: A Hierarchical Benchmark for Evaluating Bimanual Coordination of Multimodal Large Language Models

Abstract: Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) have significantly advanced embodied AI, and using them to benchmark robotic intelligence has become a pivotal trend. However, existing frameworks remain predominantly confined to single-arm manipulation, failing to capture the spatio-temporal coordination required for bimanual tasks like lifting a heavy pot. To address this, we introduce BiManiBench, a hierarchical benchmark evaluating MLLMs across three tiers: fundamental spatial reasoning, high-level action planning, and low-level end-effector control. Our framework isolates unique bimanual challenges, such as arm reachability and kinematic constraints, thereby distinguishing perceptual hallucinations from planning failures. Analysis of over 30 state-of-the-art models reveals that despite high-level reasoning proficiency, MLLMs struggle with dual-arm spatial grounding and control, frequently resulting in mutual interference and sequencing errors. These findings suggest the current paradigm lacks a deep understanding of mutual kinematic constraints, highlighting the need for future research to focus on inter-arm collision-avoidance and fine-grained temporal sequencing.

With strong perception and reasoning, these foundation models enable agents to interpret complex instructions and interact with physical environments in sophisticated ways [ 2 , 29 ] . Broadly, MLLM applications in Embodied AI span a wide spectrum, including their use as both direct controllers for autonomous agents and, crucially, as robust benchmarks for evaluating robotic performance. Early work primarily employed LLMs for task decomposition [ 42 , 41 ] , while the integration of visual modalities has extended MLLMs toward more holistic embodied control, spanning object recognition, spatial reasoning, and action generation [ 81 , 101 ] . Despite this progress, existing evaluation frameworks remain predominantly confined to single-arm manipulation scenarios. While benchmarks such as ALFWorld [ 80 ] and VLABench [ 107 ] are instrumental, they overlook bimanual capabilities essential for realistic tasks. Bimanual manipulation is more than parallel single-arm actions. It demands rigorous spatio-temporal synchronization and dynamic role assignment to prevent self-collision and kinematic singularities [ 83 ] . These complexities are further compounded by the visual hallucination inherent in MLLMs that models frequently misjudge spatial reachability or incorrectly assign arm roles, leading to immediate execution failures that single-arm benchmarks cannot detect. Consequently, the absence of a dedicated platform obscures these critical failure modes, stalling the development of versatile agents capable of human-like physical coordination. To address this gap, we introduce BiManiBench, a comprehensive benchmark for systematically evaluating MLLMs in dual-arm manipulation (Fig. 1 ). Unlike prior benchmarks that focus on a single abstraction level, BiManiBench adopts a hierarchical evaluation framework with three tiers: (1) Dual-Arm Spatial Reasoning, assessing workspace awareness and correct left-right arm assignment; (2) High-Level Action Planning, evaluating long-horizon reasoning under diverse coordination modes, including independent parallel and sequential collaborative manipulation; (3) Low-Level End-Effector Control, testing fine-grained continuous control (16-DoF) and precise bimanual synchronization. To support evaluation, we design a vision-driven agent framework with action chunking, improving efficiency and temporal coherence. To mitigate action lag, we propose a Task-Adaptive Execution Truncation mechanism, balancing open-loop efficiency with closed-loop robustness by allowing timely replanning when kinematic constraints or collisions arise. Using BiManiBench, we evaluate over 30 state-of-the-art MLLMs, including proprietary models ( e.g. , GPT-5, Gemini-2.5-Pro, Claude-4-sonnet) and open-source models ( e.g. , InternVL3, Qwen2.5-VL). Our results reveal three key findings: (1) Spatial Reasoning is Fragile: Despite general spatial awareness, stochastic hallucinations frequently disrupt precise arm allocation, causing immediate execution failures even when the high-level plan is correct. (2) Control Lags Behind Planning: While capable of high-level strategic reasoning, MLLMs struggle with the precise, continuous vision-based control required for dual-arm actuation. (3) Information Overload in Limited-Capacity Models: We find that incorporating auxiliary visual inputs is not a universal remedy for occlusion. While state-of-the-art models successfully leverage multi-view data to disambiguate complex scenes, smaller models lack the requisite reasoning bandwidth to process multiple visual streams concurrently. Consequently, the additional visual input introduces distracting noise that disrupts their decision-making process, causing a paradoxical drop in success rates compared to single-view settings. To bridge the gaps, future research should focus on integrating inter-arm kinematic constraints and specialized action heads for collision-free temporal sequencing. Our contributions can be summarized as threefold: (1) We propose BiManiBench, the first hierarchical benchmark tailored to evaluating MLLMs in dual-arm manipulation. (2) We introduce a principled evaluation protocol and a taxonomy of bimanual coordination modes, together with a vision-driven agent framework featuring adaptive execution. (3) We provide a comprehensive empirical analysis of modern MLLMs, identifying key failure modes and outlining future directions for multi-arm embodied agents. 2 Related Work 2.1 MLLMs for Embodied Agents The integration of Large Language Models (LLMs) into embodied systems has evolved from high-level semantic planning to fine-grained low-level control. Early approaches leveraged LLMs purely as semantic planners [ 41 , 42 , 102 , 106 , 105 ] , decomposing abstract instructions into primitives executed by pre-defined skills. With the advent of Multimodal LLMs (MLLMs), agents have gained the ability to process visual feedback directly. Recent frameworks, such as those evaluated in EmbodiedBench [ 100 ] , demonstrate that while MLLMs excel at high-level semantic tasks ( e.g. , object identification, reasoning), they struggle significantly with low-level continuous control and spatial grounding [ 101 , 45 ] . Unlike prior works that use MLLMs to generate code policies [ 54 ] or value functions [ 43 ] , our work evaluates the MLLM’s ability to act as a unified ”brain” that simultaneously manages high-level coordination and low-level trajectory constraints in dual-arm settings. 2.2 Benchmarks for Robotic Manipulation Benchmarking plays a pivotal role in standardizing progress in embodied intelligence. Existing benchmarks can be categorized by their task scope and primary objective: High-Level and Single-Arm Benchmarks. Early benchmarks primarily focused on symbolic reasoning. Environments such as VirtualHome [ 76 ] and ALFWorld [ 80 ] evaluate an agent’s ability to decompose instructions but abstract away physical actuation. Subsequent work shifted toward physics-grounded manipulation, including VLMbench [ 109 ] , VLABench [ 107 ] , and the manipulation subset of EmbodiedBench [ 100 ] . While these platforms evaluate perception-to-action pipelines, they are predominantly designed for single-arm manipulation and fail to capture inter-arm constraints—such as collision avoidance and synchronized holding—which are critical for realistic deployment. Bimanual Manipulation Benchmarks. To address dual-arm complexities, specialized environments have emerged. TWIN [ 35 ] introduces bimanual tasks with varying degrees of coupling but focuses primarily on Reinforcement Learning (RL) and Imitation Learning (IL). More recently, RoboTwin [ 19 , 71 ] introduced a scalable data generation framework with extensive domain randomization to facilitate the training of robust visuomotor policies ( e.g. , ACT). It offers a rich suite of high-fidelity tasks built on SAPIEN [ 98 ] . While we leverage the high-quality task assets and interaction APIs provided by RoboTwin as our simulation backend, our objective differs fundamentally. Whereas RoboTwin and TWIN are designed to generate data for training policies from scratch, BiManiBench is tailored to evaluate the zero-shot reasoning and planning capabilities of off-the-shelf MLLMs. 2.3 Challenges in Dual-Arm Coordination Beyond benchmark design, prior studies have analyzed the intrinsic difficulties of dual-arm manipulation. Dual-arm manipulation introduces complexity classes distinct from single-arm settings, necessitating precise spatio-temporal coordination [ 83 ] . Taxonomy of Coordination: As categorized in TWIN [ 35 ] , bimanual tasks range from decoupled parallel execution ( e.g. , picking two separate objects) to tightly coupled physical collaboration ( e.g. , handing over an object or collaborative lifting). The MLLM Gap: While traditional control theory addresses these via inverse kinematics and motion planning, MLLM-based agents face unique challenges. Primary among these is the spatial hallucination problem [ 55 ] , where models misinterpret the relative positions of left and right end-effectors in egocentric views. Furthermore, MLLMs often struggle to maintain ”role consistency” over long horizons [ 93 ] , inadvertently switching acting arms during synchronized tasks. Our benchmark isolates these failure modes, moving beyond simple success rates to analyze specific deficits in coordination reasoning. 3 Problem Formulation We employ two distinct scoring protocols tailored to the nature of the tasks: Success Rate (SR) for Manipulation Tasks: For both High-Level Action Planning and Low-Level End-Effector Control tasks, we report the standard Success Rate. To better evaluate MLLMs’ capabilities in dual-arm manipulation, we enhanced specific dual-arm tasks within existing simulators and designed additional scenarios to support comprehensive assessments. Below is an introduction to the three different task forms. Dual-Arm Spatial Reasoning. Dual-Arm Spatial Reasoning assesses the agent’s fundamental spatial awareness. Given a visual observation of the workspace, the model must determine the optimal manipulator (left or right arm) to execute a grasp. This evaluation is motivated by a critical challenge distinct from single-arm manipulation: the necessity for dynamic arm assignment. In expansive workspaces ( e.g. , a long tabletop), each manipulator operates under strict kinematic constraints and limited reachability. Consequently, incorrect arm selection is not merely a suboptimal strategy but a potential failure mode; forcing a grasp with the wrong arm can lead to kinematic singularities, unreachable targets, or physical collisions, thereby degrading execution performance and compromising hardware safety. Thus, the ability to accurately map spatial locations to the appropriate effector is a prerequisite for robust bimanual control. High-Level Action Planning. High-Level Action Planning evaluates the agent’s logical reasoning and decomposition capabilities in long-horizon tasks. The model must act as a strategic planner and be responsible for outputting a logical sequence of atomic action primitives ( e.g. , categorizing objects into containers).
